# Vector spaces

## Introduction and definitions

A vector space is a mathematical structure that offers very interesting properties. The main element of a vector space is called a vector. We say that it is a structure because you can use operations between this vectors

:::definition Vector space

Let $E$ be a non empty set, with two operations : an internal operation (or law) called addition and noted $+$:

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
  E \times E & \longrightarrow & E \\
  (u, v) & \longrightarrow & u + v
\end{array}
$$

and an external operation (or law) called *multiplication* and noted $\cdot$ :

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
  \mathbb{K} \times E & \longrightarrow & E \\
  (\lambda, u) & \longrightarrow & \lambda \cdot u
\end{array}
$$

We say that $(E, +, \cdot)$ is a vector space on $\mathbb{K}$ or a $\mathbb{K}$-vector space if the following properties are satisfied :

- The addition on $E$ is :
    1. commutative : $u + v = v + u$ for all $(u, v) \in \mathbb{K}^2$
    2. associative : $(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)$ for all $(u, v, w) \in \mathbb{K}^3$
    3. own a "zero" called null vector : there exists $\vec{0}$ such that $\vec{u} + \vec{0} = \vec{u}$, for all $\vec{u} \in E$
    4. Every element has an opposite : for all $u \in E$, there is an element denoted $-u$ such that $u + (-u) = 0$
- External multiplication verifies :
    1. $1 \cdot u = u$ pour tout $u \in E$
    2. $\lambda \cdot (\mu \cdot u) = (\lambda\mu) \cdot u$ for all $(\lambda, \mu) \in \mathbb{K}^2, u \in E$
- Multiplication is distributive over addition :
    1. $\lambda \cdot (u + v) = \lambda \cdot u + \lambda \cdot v$ for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{K}$, for all $(u, v) \in E^2$
    2. $(\lambda + \mu) \cdot u = \lambda \cdot u + \mu \cdot u$ for all $(\lambda, \mu) \in \mathbb{K}^2$, for all $u \in E$
:::

:::proposition Vector space axioms

If $(E, +, \cdot)$ is a $\mathbb{K}$-vector space, then :
1. Its null vector is unique
2. Every vector of $E$ has a unique opposite
3. $\lambda \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{K}$
4. $0 \cdot u = 0$ for all $u \in E$
5. If $\lambda \cdot u = 0$ then $\lambda = 0$ or $u = 0$
6. $-u = (-1) \cdot u$ for all $u \in E$

:::

:::proof

Let $x \in \mathbb{R}$ be the redondant value.
